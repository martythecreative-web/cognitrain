The Science Behind
Your Training

Every mode in CogiTrain targets a specific cognitive domain — measurable, trainable, and directly connected to how well you think, decide, and perform under pressure.

🧠 N-Back Working Memory
What it is

A letter appears on screen every few seconds. Your job: press Match if the current letter is the same as the one that appeared N steps ago. N increases as you improve — 1-back is easy, 4-back is genuinely hard.

What it trains

Working memory — your brain's mental whiteboard. It's the system that holds and manipulates information in the moment: keeping a phone number in mind, following multi-step instructions, or tracking what was said 3 sentences ago in a conversation.

Research spotlight: N-Back training was used in studies by Jaeggi et al. (2008) showing improvements in fluid intelligence — the ability to reason through novel problems. It's been adopted in military pilot training programs and elite academic prep.
Real-world benefits

Better recall during conversations · Improved ability to multitask without losing track · Stronger reading comprehension · Reduced mental fatigue during complex work

What your score means

Reaching Level 4+ (2-back) puts you in the top half of adults. Level 7+ (3-back) is elite territory. Your mean RT at high N-back levels is a direct measure of working memory bandwidth.

🛑 Go / No-Go Inhibitory Control
What it is

A circle appears. Green = tap immediately. Red = do NOT tap. Sounds simple — but at higher levels the timing window tightens and the red/green ratio shifts, making impulsive mistakes much easier.

What it trains

Inhibitory control — your brain's ability to suppress an automatic response. This is the executive function responsible for not saying the wrong thing, resisting distractions, and stopping yourself from acting on impulse.

Research spotlight: Go/No-Go tasks are the gold standard in ADHD research and impulse control studies. Studies show regular inhibition training correlates with reduced impulsivity scores and improved sustained attention in both clinical and healthy populations.
Real-world benefits

Fewer impulsive decisions · Stronger ability to stay on task · Improved emotional regulation · Better performance in high-stakes, fast-moving situations

What your score means

Commission errors (tapping red) reveal impulsivity. Omission errors (missing green) reveal inattention. A strong score has near-zero commission errors — that's the harder skill to train.

⚡ Choice Reaction Time Processing Speed
What it is

A flash appears on the Left or Right side. Tap the correct button as fast as possible. The foreperiod (wait time before the flash) varies randomly to prevent anticipation — you can't cheat it by rhythmic tapping.

What it trains

Processing speed — how fast your brain perceives, decides, and acts. Unlike simple reaction time (just reacting), choice RT requires identification and decision-making, measuring your neural transmission efficiency.

Research spotlight: Processing speed is one of the strongest predictors of overall cognitive performance and is one of the first metrics to decline with age. Studies by Jensen (1982) established RT as a proxy for general intelligence (g-factor). It's used in sports science to assess athletic readiness.
Real-world benefits

Faster decisions under pressure · Quicker reflexes while driving · Sharper performance in sports and gaming · Reduced cognitive lag in complex tasks

What your score means

Average choice RT for healthy adults is 350–450ms. Under 300ms is exceptional. Your SD (variability) matters as much as your mean — consistent fast responses indicate stable neural processing, not just lucky fast trials.

🔀 Task Switch Cognitive Flexibility
What it is

A number appears and you're asked one of two rules: ODD or EVEN? or HIGH (>5) or LOW (≤5)? The rule switches randomly. Switch trials — where you have to change rules mid-task — are always harder than repeat trials.

What it trains

Cognitive flexibility — your ability to switch mental gears, update your approach, and disengage from one way of thinking to adopt another. This is the foundation of adaptability, creative problem-solving, and avoiding mental ruts.

Research spotlight: The switch cost (extra time on switch vs. repeat trials) is a validated biomarker for prefrontal cortex efficiency. Elite performers across fields — chess grandmasters, surgeons, fighter pilots — show significantly lower switch costs than average. Your 6ms switch cost, Marty, is in the top percentile.
Real-world benefits

Better at context-switching between tasks · Reduced friction when plans change unexpectedly · Stronger creative problem-solving · Faster recovery from interruptions at work

What your score means

Switch cost under 100ms is good. Under 50ms is strong. Under 20ms is elite. Reducing your switch cost over time is a direct measure of prefrontal cortex training. Accuracy on switch trials specifically — not just overall — is the key metric to watch.

🎨 Stroop Cognitive Control
What it is

A color word (like RED) appears in a mismatched ink color (like green). Your job is to tap the ink color — not the word. Congruent trials (word matches ink) are easy. Incongruent trials (mismatch) force your brain to override a deeply automatic reading response.

What it trains

Cognitive control and interference suppression — your brain's ability to override a dominant automatic response in favor of a less automatic one. This is the same mental muscle used when you resist checking your phone mid-task, stay focused while distracted, or hold back a reaction you know is wrong.

Research spotlight: The Stroop effect has been replicated thousands of times since J. Ridley Stroop's original 1935 study. It's used clinically to assess frontal lobe function, in sports psychology for attentional control training, and in aging research as an early marker of cognitive decline. Reducing your congruency effect (RT gap between easy and hard trials) is a direct measure of improving executive control.
Real-world benefits

Stronger focus in distracting environments · Faster resolution of conflicting information · Better control over impulsive reactions · Improved performance on any task requiring selective attention

What your score means

Your congruency effect (reaction time on mismatched trials minus matched trials) is the key metric. Under 100ms is good. Under 50ms is strong. Under 30ms is elite. Overall accuracy on incongruent trials specifically — not just total accuracy — is what to watch as you improve.

🔬 Why train all five?

Working memory, inhibitory control, processing speed, cognitive flexibility, and cognitive control are the five pillars of executive function — the brain systems housed in the prefrontal cortex that govern how you think, decide, and perform under pressure.

Training them together produces transfer effects — improvements that show up in daily life, not just in the training tasks. Athletes call this cross-training. Neuroscientists call it near and far transfer. You'll call it noticing you're sharper.

Important note: CogiTrain is a cognitive training tool, not a medical device. Consistent training over weeks and months produces the best results. Treat it like the gym — one session won't transform you, but showing up regularly will.
📅 How Much Should You Train?
The research-backed minimum

Studies on cognitive training consistently show that 15–20 minutes per day, 4–5 days per week, for at least 4 weeks is the threshold where real-world transfer effects start appearing — meaning improvements that show up in your actual life, not just in the app.

A realistic daily session

Pick 2–3 modes per day and rotate. You don't need to play all five every session. A good rotation: N-Back + Go/No-Go one day, Choice RT + Task Switch the next, Stroop any day as a warm-up. Your brain benefits more from varied stimulation than grinding the same mode repeatedly.
